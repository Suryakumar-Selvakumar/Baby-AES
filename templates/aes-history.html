AES History

1. The Need for a New Standard

In the late 1990s, it became clear that the existing standard for encryption, the Data Encryption Standard (DES), was no longer secure against increasingly sophisticated attacks and growing computational power.

2. The Competition

In 1997, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) announced a competition to select a successor to DES.

The competition was open to the international cryptographic community and required that the new standard be a symmetric-key block cipher capable of protecting sensitive government information.

3. The Requirements

Proposed algorithms needed to support block sizes of 128 bits and key sizes of 128, 192, and 256 bits.

Submissions were evaluated on security, performance, efficiency, implementability, and flexibility.

4. Submissions and Testing

Fifteen algorithms from various international proposers were submitted; these included MARS, RC6, Serpent, Twofish, and Rijndael, among others.

Several rounds of analysis and testing were conducted, including public scrutiny and comment periods to ensure transparency and community involvement.

5. The Selection

In October 2000, NIST selected the Rijndael cipher, designed by two Belgian cryptographers, Vincent Rijmen and Joan Daemen, as the winner of the competition.

Rijndael was chosen for its combination of security, performance, efficiency, and flexibility in both hardware and software environments.

6. Standardization

In 2001, NIST released Federal Information Processing Standards Publication 197 (FIPS PUB 197), which officially announced AES and detailed the Rijndael algorithm specification as the chosen standard.

7. Adoption and Usage

AES became effective as a federal government standard on May 26, 2002, after approval by the Secretary of Commerce.

AES is used worldwide and is one of the most popular algorithms for symmetric key encryption.

It is also part of the ISO/IEC 18033-3 international standard.

8. Enduring Legacy

AES has stood the test of time, remaining secure against cryptanalytic attacks better than any other block cipher of its time.

It is implemented in various protocols like Secure Sockets Layer (SSL)/Transport Layer Security (TLS), Wi-Fi Protected Access (WPA2), and many others.

AES is mandatory in many government cryptographic solutions and is widely used in the private sector.

9. Ongoing Research

While AES remains secure, cryptographers continue to study its theoretical underpinnings and resistance to attack, particularly in the face of quantum computing advances.

Side-channel attacks and other non-direct cryptanalytic attacks are an area of ongoing research and concern.

10. Future Prospects

AES will likely be replaced in the future as computational capacities increase and new cryptographic challenges arise.

NIST has begun a post-quantum cryptography standardization process, looking ahead to the era of quantum computing.
